Introduction

Synthetic control methods is a causal inference approach that uses data simulation to create a synthesized counterfactual comparison against observed units that received treatment. A great advantage of these methods is that treatment effects can be estimated on as few as one unit, and use a weighted combination of the closest matches among non-treated units for a stronger comparison. Therefore, SCM creates a ceteris paribus comparison, even if a close match does not exist in reality.

This tutorial will discuss two different synthetic control methods for causal inference in the context of a 2024 study by Letellier et al., “Applying a two-stage generalized synthetic control approach to quantify the heterogeneous health effects of extreme weather events”. In this study, the authors identify a causal effect of exposure to particulate matter (PM2.5) from wildfire smoke on respiratory health across several California counties. The published paper can be accessed here.

Synthetic Control Method (SCM) Approach

To show a causal effect of a defined event, such as a wildfire or climate policy (also known as a “treatment”), a credible counterfactual can show what might have happened in the absence of such treatment. Traditional causal inference methods, such as difference-in-difference or regression discontinuity designs, require a large sample size of treated and non-treated units and are limited to the quality of existing observational data. SCMs are advantageous because they can compare as few as one treated unit to an optimized synthetic counterfactual constructed from a pool of untreated units (the “donor pool”). Once a robust counterfactual is created, the difference post-treatment between the treated unit(s) and the non-treated counterfactual is the Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT).

Key Assumptions of an SCM Approach

There are three key assumptions to check when using any SCM. These are non-negotiable when estimating a causal effect. Assumption 1: Strong Pre-Treatment Fit In the period before the treatment, does the synthetic data closely follow the treated-unit observed data? Assumption 2: Transparent Donor Pool with No Spillover Are there any other wildfires that are contributing PM2.5? Assumption 3: No Coinciding Shocks Is there any treatment in the untreated units? Does the treatment of one unit affect the treatment of another unit?

Let’s explore each assumption in the context of the study below, and how to use robustness checks and data visualizations to determine if the assumptions are met.

2 Different SCM Approaches

A basic SCM estimates the effect of a treatment on a single unit, and uses a sizeable donor pool to construct the synthetic counterfactual. In R, the package {tidysynth} weights and creates the synthetic data set, compare observed (treated) versus expected (synthetic) data patterns, and visualize the results and robustness checks.

Another form of SCM, known as Generalized Synthetic Control Methods (GSCMs), estimate the effect of a treatment on several units independently, where the same donor pool creates separate weighted counterfactuals for each unit. The R package {gsynth} estimates the ATT of several units simultaneously and summed, and visualizes the results.

Note

The original study used GSCM for the analysis, but this tutorial will demonstrate the use of both SCM via {tidysynth} and GSCM via {gsynth}.

Study Background

Letellier et al. (2024) utilizes three major November 2018 wildfires in close succession as a quasi-experimental opportunity to test the effects of smoke particulate matter on respiratory health. * The unit of analysis is the county. * The treatment is exposure to at least 10 micrograms per cubic meter of wildfire-smoke particulate matter <2.5 micrometers (“PM2.5”). Exposure to less than 10 micrograms per cubic meter is considered unexposed. * The outcome is measured by the total number of hospitalizations for respiratory diagnoses per county.

All 58 California counties were included in the analysis: 27 exposed counties were compared to 27 synthetic counterfactuals using weighted inputs from the 31 non-exposed counties. The pre-exposure period with no notable wildfire smoke is defined as the 7 weeks between 9/13/2018 and 11/7/2018 (weeks 37- 44), and the exposure period was 11/8/2018 to 12/5/2018 (weeks 45- 48).

Note

The mention of the “two-stage” GSCM in the study refers to a meta-regression analysis that the authors conducted after the GSCM to parse out any differences in ATT across socioeconomic or demographic groups. For clarity, this tutorial will focus on the first-stage GSCM analysis only. More information regarding the second-stage methods can be found in the study’s published GitHub (below).

Variables

CA_hosp_County a proxy panel data set provided in the Letellier 2018 paper, Applying a two-stage generalized synthetic control approach to quantify the heterogeneous health effects of extreme weather events, describing the effects of the California 2018 wildfires. The data includes 58 counties (27 treated, 31 untreated). The thirty one untreated variables are the donor pool used to create the synthetic control. Each row represents a unique date, county-level observations which includes the following variables:

  • Treatment variable: smoke the concentration (μg/m3) of wildfire-specific PM2.5, which will be used to define the actual treatment assignment smokebin during data processing. - (smokebin is a binary value defined by whether the average smoke is above or below the threshold of 10 μg/m3)

  • Outcome variables: resp: the count of daily respiratory hospitalizations

  • Covariates: maximum temperature (K) tmax , precipitation (mm) prec, humidity (g/m^3) hum, shortwave radiation (W/m^2) shrtwv_rad, and wind (m/s).

Data Processing

Code 
# [1] df with avg smoke per county during exposure period ONLY 
county_smoke_exp <- CA_hosp_County |> 
  
  filter(week >= 45 & week < 49) |> # exposure period
  
  # avg smoke by county
  group_by(COUNTY_1) |> 
  summarize(smoke_week45_49 = mean(smoke), 
            post_countyname = unique(countyname), 
            state = "CA") |> 
  ungroup() |>
  
  # define treatment variable
  mutate(smokebin = smoke_week45_49 >= 10)

# [2] df summarizing covariates for each observation for treatment and donor group
CA_hosp_covar <- CA_hosp_County |> 
  
  filter(week >= 37 & week < 49) |> # study period
  
  left_join(county_smoke_exp, by = "COUNTY_1") |> # joins smokebin 
  
  # summarizes covariates
  group_by(week, COUNTY_1, countyname, smokebin) |> 
  summarize(
    resp = sum(resp), # num respiratory hospitalizations
    tmax = mean(tmax), # avg temperature
    prec = mean(prec), # avg precipitation 
    hum = mean(hum), # avg humidity
    shrtwv_rad = mean(shrtwv_rad), # avg shortwave radiation
    wind = mean(wind)) |> # avg wind
  ungroup()

# [3] adds variable exp_weekly, used by gsynth to look at smokebin only 
CA_hosp_covar$exp_weekly <- ifelse(CA_hosp_covar$smokebin==TRUE & CA_hosp_covar$week>=45,1,0)

This data processing takes the panel data CA_hosp_County and produces the data frame CA_hosp_covar. The resulting data frame includes the newly defined the treatment variable smokebin and exp_weekly which formats this variable to be interpretable by the {gsynth} algorithm. The data frame also includes the relevant measure (mean or total) of each of the covariates across each county.

In order to define the treatment variable smokebin [1], the original data frame was used to summarize the average PM2.5 levels during only the exposure period for each county smoke_week45_49. From there, smokebin was assigned 1 or 0 based on whether the smoke_week45_49 met the threshold of 10 μg/m3. These values were then joined to values in the study period in CA_hosp_covar which summarized the covariates across each week-county observation.

Finally, variable exp_weekly was defined as 1 if the observation was both within the exposure period and for a county within the treatment group (using smokebin) [3].

The study’s original code (found in the GitHub repository) processed the data differently, splitting the original data frame into multiple new data frames based on the study period, pre-exposure period, and post-exposure period. This tutorial used various functions from the tidyverse package to achieve the same data frames with fewer intermediate steps.

Simple Synthetic Control Method

Using the {tidysynth} package, the function synthetic_control() is implemented to create a synthetic control from a weighted donor pool for the one treated county, “Alameda”. One limitation of this function is that synthetic control creation is limited to one treated unit at a time. To reach an ATT value similar to one achieved in Letellier et al. 2018 using the gsynth() function that we will explore later, an individual ATT would have to be computed via a separate synthetic control for each county. The functions synthetic_control(), generate_predictor(), generate_weights(), and generate_control() are used in tandem with parameters from the dataframe CA_hosp_covar to create a closest match synthetic copy of Alameda County.

Code 
# coercing classes for simple synthetic control
covar_simp_synth <-CA_hosp_covar
covar_simp_synth$week <- as.integer(covar_simp_synth$week)
covar_simp_synth$COUNTY_1 <- as.character(covar_simp_synth$COUNTY_1)
covar_simp_synth$smokebin<- as.integer(covar_simp_synth$smokebin)
covar_simp_synth$resp<- as.numeric(covar_simp_synth$resp)


# running the synthetic control
scm_fire_data <- covar_simp_synth %>%
  synthetic_control(
    outcome = resp,# outcome variable resp hospitalizations
    unit = countyname, #unit identifier
    time = week, #time identifier
    i_unit = "Alameda", #treated unit
    i_time = 45, #intervention (treatment) year
    generate_placebos = TRUE) %>% #generate placebo test for all states

    # defining characteristics synthetic county must match
    generate_predictor(  
    time_window = 37:44, #pre treatment period only to compute predictors 
    pre_resp = mean(resp, na.rm = TRUE), #avg pre-treat hospitalizations
    tmax = mean(tmax, na.rm = TRUE), #avg max temperature
    prec = mean(prec, na.rm = TRUE), #avg precipitation
    hum = mean(hum, na.rm = TRUE),  #avg humidity
    shrtwv_rad = mean(shrtwv_rad, na.rm = TRUE),
    wind = mean(wind, na.rm = TRUE)) %>% #avg pre treatment outcome variable
  
  # take the predictor summaries from above and differential weight each donor state to create the best synthetic county from the donor counties
  generate_weights(
    optimization_window = 37:44, #period over which donor weights are optimized
    margin_ipop = 0.02, #interior point method , smaller margin ipop is a preciseness indicator, smaller = more precise
    sigf_ipop = 7, # how many sig figs to use 
    bound_ipop = 6) %>% 
  generate_control()

As a preliminary step to running the synthetic control, input data frame classes are manipulated to ensure they align with later visualizations. Within the synthetic_control() function, the first section specifies the outcome variable of interest, identifying the unit, time object, and intervention time. Within the next function, generate_predictor(), all the control variables used to create the synthetic controls and the time window from which to compute them are defined. Finally, the donors, which are the untreated counties used to create the control for Alameda County, are weighted using generate_weights, and preciseness of the weight calculator is specified.

The Average Treatment Effect on the Treated is determined by evaluating the post-treatment difference between the synthetic control and the measured values in Alameda County. In this example, using the fictive data, the ATT is calculated to be -166.95, indicating that wildfire presence in the county caused ~167 less hospitalizations. However, A good synthetic control should mirror pre-intervention trends closely, and the potential pre-treatment fit and common shocks assumption violation by the synthetic control indicate that causal estimation using synthetic_control() for Alameda County should be considered with a high degree of skepticism.

Synthetic control methods using the {tidysynth} package are useful when analyzing a single treated unit along with a large donor pool of untreated units. The larger the donor pool, the more opportunities there are to create a close synthetic match. Next, the generalized synthetic control method will be evaluated.

Generalized Synthetic Control Method

For this study, the authors analyzed their data using {gsynth}, a generalized synthetic control method that can be used across multiple treatments via an interactive fixed effects model. Unlike a traditional synthetic control method, which aggregates the treatment groups and composes one counterfactual, GSCM creates a counterfactual for each treated (exposed) county. These counterfactuals predict what would have happened in the respective county had the 2018 fires not occurred.

Generalized Synthetic Control Model

Code 
# [1] Preparing indexing for iterative {gsynth}

# indices of exposed counties
exposed_counties_ind <- unique(CA_hosp_covar$COUNTY_1[CA_hosp_covar$smokebin == TRUE])
# names of exposed counties
exp_county_nam <- unique(CA_hosp_covar$countyname[CA_hosp_covar$smokebin == TRUE])
# number of exposed counties
n_exposed_counties <- length(exposed_counties_ind)

# indices of exposed counties
not_exposed_counties_ind <- unique(CA_hosp_covar$COUNTY_1[CA_hosp_covar$smokebin == FALSE])

# [2] Preparing empty lists to update results and figures into for each county

results_list <- list()
gap_plot <- list()
trend_plot <- list()

# [3] Generalized synthetic control method for each county
for (i in 1:n_exposed_counties) {
  
  county_i <- exposed_counties_ind[i] # county of interest
  
  # [3a] df of a treated unit i and the donor pool based on CA_hosp_covar
  test_CA_hosp_covar_ind2 <- c(county_i, not_exposed_counties_ind)
  test_CA_hosp_covar <- CA_hosp_covar[CA_hosp_covar$COUNTY_1 %in% test_CA_hosp_covar_ind2, ]

  # [3b] runs generalized synthetic control 
  gsynth.out_1 <- gsynth(resp ~ exp_weekly 
                       + tmax 
                       + prec 
                       + hum 
                       + shrtwv_rad 
                       + wind, 
                       
                       data = test_CA_hosp_covar, # [3a]
                       index = c("countyname","week"), 
                       nboots = 100, 
                       inference = "parametric", 
                       se = TRUE, 
                       parallel = FALSE)

  # [3c] update {gsynth} results for county_i into list
  
  # get results of county_i
  county_nam <- exp_county_nam[i]
  ATT_1<-gsynth.out_1$att.avg
  SE_1<-gsynth.out_1$est.avg[,2]
  
  # adds county_i to list of results
  results_list[[i]] <- cbind(county_nam, ATT_1, SE_1) 
  
  # [3d] updates figures into lists of plots with a plot for each county
  
  # adds county_i gap plot to list of gap plots for each county
  gap_plot[[i]]<-plot(gsynth.out_1, type = "gap" , title = county_nam, xlab = "Week", ylab="ATT")
  
  # adds county_i trends plot to list of gap plots for each county
  trend_plot[[i]] <- plot(gsynth.out_1, type = "counterfactual",  title = county_nam, xlab = "Week", ylab="Average Hospitalizations")
  
  }

In order to construct each counterfactual, the model is initialized [1,2] and iteratively runs for each exposed county. Each iteration additionally updates variables results_list, gap_plots, and trend_plots with the respective values and figures for each county [3c, 3d].

The model runs on a data frame test_CA_hosp_covar constructed from observations from one exposed county county_i and the 31 nonexposed counties [3a]. The model uses the nonexposed counties, indicated by a zero value of exp_weekly, to form the donor pool. Each iteration of {gsynth} creates a synthetic control for ‘county_i’. It takes resp as the outcome with the treatment exp_weekly and covariates as predictors in the factor‑model framework [3b]. In this process, donor pool counties are assigned weights based on their alignment with county_i on the predictors: shortwave radiation, humidity, precipitation, mean daily respiratory hospitalizations, and mean max temperature.

The resulting average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) compares the number of respiratory hospitalizations during the exposure period in the county_i with the resulting synthetic control. The ATTs of each county are collated in results_list [3c].

To evaluate the validity of each generated synthetic control, the author used a gap plot and trends plot to evaluate the matching success of the synthetic counterfactual with the observed data for each treated county. The gap plots show the ATT to visualize these differences on the y axis, with relative weeks (pre and post treatment) on the x axis. Similarly, the trend plot shows average hospitalizations for both the synthetic control and treated unit over the course of the study. The standard error for both the gap plot and the trend plot is visualized by error bars [3c, 3d].

Evaluating Assumptions of the Study Design

Let’s review the assumptions for both synthetic control methods that must be met when estimating a causal effect, in the context of this study’s design.

Assumption 1: Strong Pre-Treatment Fit

Synthetic control methods rely on a strong synthetic counterfactual to compare with the observed data of the treated unit. The data pre-exposure to wildfire smoke should be very close, and overlapping is even better. The study’s Figure S4 provides a closer look at a gap plot and trends plot for Santa Clara County (one of the counties with a significant ATT). Both the lefthand gap plot hovering around zero pre-exposure and the righthand trends plot with similar trendlines suggest that there is good pre-treatment fit for this county and its synthetic counterfactual. The study’s Figure S5 provides 27 gap plots for all of the exposed counties, but including the trends plots would provide even more evidence.

Though this does not affect pre-exposure data, one strength in this GCSM is the use of fixed effects to control for differences in meteorological conditions. Fixed effects reduce the potential for omitted variable bias that can confound our treatment estimate.

Assumption 2: Transparent Donor Pool with No Spillover

Another key assumption for synthetic control methods is that there is no exposure in counties considered “non-exposed” in the donor pool. In this study, the donor pool comprised 31 counties that experienced less than 10 μg/m3 of PM2.5 on average during the exposure period. This is a sizable assortment of similar administrative units within the state of California that should have similar state-level policy and social bounds, and are spatially representative of the entire state. However, the basis of the 10 μg/m3 threshold is unclear — the mean smoke exposure in some ZIP code tabulation areas exceeds 71 μg/m3 during the study period. Generalizing the mean smoke exposure across the entire county can yield biased ATT estimates that are not applicable to any single ZIP code. The analysis could be strengthened by conducting a sensitivity analysis comparing different exposure thresholds to see whether the estimated ATT changes.

In addition to the continuous, heterogeneous spread of smoke, individuals also do not necessarily abide by county lines. For individuals living close to the edge of their county, the preferred or closest hospital may be in a different county in which they live. These “edge effects” near the borders are a common way that spillover can bias estimates in any context. In the specific context of a wildfire, people may move even more than expected – individuals may evacuate their homes from the approaching fire or may voluntarily leave the area (across county lines) to escape the smoky air. It is reasonable to assume spillover in this context because the counties that contained the fires were included in the exposed (Butte) and unexposed (Ventura, Los Angeles) groups. In cases like this with significant spillover chances, consider dropping units from analysis where the fires occurred or nearby regions to create a “spillover buffer”.

Assumption 3: No Coinciding Shocks

To reduce confounding of the causal estimate, there should be no other factors or events that could alter the outcome in the same manner as the treatment. In this study, three different large wildfires were included, therefore capturing all concurrent shocks into the treatment. Figure S3 from the study suggests that no other coinciding shocks occurred during the wildfire smoke event, but including data from weeks 37-41 in the figure would strengthen the acceptance of this assumption.

Other Considerations

To apply these findings to any public health management or policy, the scope of any identified causal effects here is limited by a few key characteristics of the study design.

Because of the short measurement period included, it would be difficult to extrapolate anything about the long-term impacts of smoke PM2.5 on health. This study provides the most information about acute respiratory distress caused by increased levels of PM2.5.

In addition, using hospitalization counts as a measure of health can introduce sampling bias. This measure leaves out individuals who experienced smoke-exposure impacts but only visited an outpatient facility (i.e., their primary care provider or a telehealth visit) because their symptoms did not warrant hospitalization. There may also be selection bias in this measurement, as individuals with lower socioeconomic status or those in counties with fewer proximate hospitals (such as rural counties) may be less inclined to visit a hospital due to high out-of-pocket costs and long travel times, unless it is an emergency. Communities with a higher proportion of low-income or rural residents may be underrepresented in these estimates.

Wrap Up

This tutorial has explored synthetic control methods as an approach to causal inference of quasi-random natural events or public policies. Both the standard SCM and Generalized SCM provide the tools to evaluate the effect of a treatment on as few as one unit, provided that there is a sizeable, high-quality donor pool of unexposed units with which to build a synthetic counterfactual.

R packages such as {tidysynth} or {gsynth} can construct the synthetic counterfactual, and control for weights and fixed effects can test the robustness of the synthetic counterfactual and the estimated Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT).

To use an SCM, the observed and synthetic data must closely align pre-treatment, the synthetic data must be free of treatment spillover, and there cannot be any other events or policies concurrently that could bias our estimate.

Ultimately, Letellier et al demonstrate the complex nature of study design with real-world events and data; the most important part of any SCM is to critically evaluate the context of the study and the nuances of the case at hand. This study is a great example of how synthetic control methods can be used to help solve pressing environmental and public health issues.
